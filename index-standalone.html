Mobile App · Health & Wellness · 2023

Bloom Health

A gamified wellness tracking app that makes healthy habits feel effortless, rewarding, and genuinely human.

My Role Lead UX/UI Designer
Timeline 14 Weeks
Tools Figma, Maze, Miro, Lottie
Platform iOS & Android
The Challenge

Health apps that feel like homework

Most wellness apps are either too clinical — cold dashboards packed with numbers — or too gamified in ways that feel hollow and patronising. Users start with enthusiasm and abandon the app within two weeks.

Bloom's goal was different: create an app that felt like a supportive friend, not a fitness tracker. One that celebrated progress without shaming setbacks.

"How do we make people feel good about their health journey, even on the days they fall short?"

Deliverables
  • User research synthesis
  • Persona development
  • Information architecture
  • Low & high fidelity wireframes
  • Interactive prototype
  • Design system components
  • Usability test reports
Team
  • 1 Lead Designer (me)
  • 2 Product Managers
  • 4 Engineers
  • 1 Content Strategist
01 — Discover

Understanding the real story

I conducted 18 user interviews over two weeks, targeting people who had downloaded and abandoned at least two health apps in the past year. I also ran a diary study with 8 participants for 10 days to capture real-time emotional responses to habit tracking.

Alongside user research, I audited 12 competitor apps across health, fitness, and mindfulness categories — mapping their onboarding flows, notification strategies, and reward mechanics.

😩 Guilt spirals

14 of 18 participants reported feeling guilty or embarrassed when they missed days, often leading to full abandonment rather than resumption.

🎯 Goal overload

Users set 6–10 goals simultaneously at onboarding. Apps with more than 3 active habits had 3× higher drop-off rates within week one.

💬 Missing context

Users wanted to understand "why" behind recommendations — not just what to do, but the reasoning that made it meaningful to their life.

02 — Define

Who we're designing for

Research data crystallised into two primary personas that guided every subsequent design decision:

Maya, 28 — The Restart Loop

A marketing manager who re-downloads health apps every few months with high motivation, then burns out when life gets busy. She needs flexibility and encouragement, not streaks.

"I know what I should be doing. I just need something that doesn't make me feel terrible when I don't."

James, 34 — The Data Skeptic

A software engineer who tracks metrics obsessively but struggles to translate numbers into feeling better. He needs apps to help him connect data to lived experience.

"I've got years of step data and absolutely no idea if it means anything."

From these personas, I defined the core design principles that would guide Bloom:

1

Progress over perfection

Celebrate any positive action, no matter how small. Remove streaks that penalise rest.

2

Start small, grow gradually

Onboard with one habit. Unlock new goals naturally as the user builds confidence.

3

Context, not commands

Every recommendation includes the "why" — grounded in the user's own patterns.

03 — Design

From sketches to structure

I mapped the information architecture first, then moved into lo-fi wireframes — 40+ screens exploring different approaches to the home dashboard, habit logging, and progress visualisation. I tested three distinct home screen concepts with 12 participants before moving to high-fidelity.

E-Commerce · Sustainability · 2023

Verdant

A sustainable fashion marketplace that makes ethical shopping as beautiful and easy as conventional retail.

My Role UX Lead + Visual Design
Timeline 18 Weeks
Tools Figma, Adobe XD, Maze, Hotjar
Platform Web (Responsive)
The Challenge

Making ethics as easy as clicking "Add to Cart"

Sustainable fashion exists, but finding it is exhausting. Consumers have to navigate dozens of indie brand websites with inconsistent UX, no centralised search, and zero transparency on what "sustainable" actually means for a given product.

Verdant needed to solve the discovery problem — but without lecturing users. The goal was a platform that felt like ASOS for conscious shoppers, not a climate anxiety simulator.

"How do we make sustainable fashion feel aspirational and effortless, not preachy?"

Deliverables
  • Discovery & research synthesis
  • Content strategy framework
  • Full responsive design system
  • Product listing templates
  • Checkout & cart flows
  • Sustainability rating system UI
  • Brand onboarding flow
Impact
  • 40% higher conversion vs benchmark
  • 22% lower cart abandonment
  • 4.8/5 usability score
01 — Research

The real barrier to sustainable shopping

I surveyed 250 online shoppers aged 22–40 who described themselves as environmentally conscious, but whose actual sustainable purchase history was low. The gap between intention and action was the core problem.

🔍 Discovery fatigue

78% of respondents had abandoned a sustainable purchase because finding the right product took "too long". Average search session was 14 minutes before giving up.

🏷 Greenwashing distrust

64% didn't trust sustainability claims made by brands directly. They wanted third-party verification displayed clearly at point of purchase.

💸 Price justification

Sustainable items are often 30–80% more expensive. Users needed the story behind the price — the craftsmanship, materials, and fair wages — surfaced naturally.

02 — Design System

Building trust through visual language

The design system needed to communicate transparency and authenticity. I developed a "Sustainability Score" visual component — a simple 5-leaf rating visible on every product card — that gave users instant legibility without information overload.

Typography and colour choices drew from natural materials: earthy greens, warm neutrals, and selective blush pink accents to signal warmth and quality without greenwashing aesthetics.

03 — User Journey

Designing a shopping flow that educates without lecturing

1

Discover through values, not categories

Onboarding asks users to select values (Organic, Fair Trade, Local, etc.) rather than clothing categories. This filters the entire catalogue to products that align with what they actually care about.

2

Trust built into every product card

The "Verdant Score" (a 5-leaf rating) appears on every card with a tap-to-explain tooltip. Users learn what it means through interaction, not upfront education that interrupts browsing.

3

Price narrative at point of decision

A "Why this price?" accordion on product pages breaks down cost into materials, labour, and brand margin — giving users the story they need to feel good about spending more.

4

Carbon offset built into checkout

A single checkbox in checkout offsets shipping emissions for $0.40. Default on. Opt-out if needed. 89% of beta users kept it checked.

Dashboard · Smart Home · 2022

Lumina

A smart home control dashboard that simplifies the complexity of connected living into a single, beautiful interface.

My Role UX/UI Designer
Timeline 20 Weeks
Tools Figma, Principle, ProtoPie
Platform iPad & Web
The Problem

Brilliant technology, terrible experience

Smart home technology has reached mainstream adoption — but the apps controlling it haven't kept pace. Users with 10+ devices navigate 4–6 different manufacturer apps, each with its own logic and interface. The promise of a "smart home" ironically makes life more complicated.

Lumina was commissioned to be the unified layer: one interface to control everything. The design challenge was as much about information architecture as it was visual design.

"How do we take 20+ smart devices and make them feel like one seamless home?"

The Challenge
  • 12+ device categories to unify
  • Real-time status across all devices
  • Scene & automation management
  • Energy monitoring integration
  • Voice control companion design
Key Metrics
  • Task completion: 94% success rate
  • Time-on-task reduced by 58%
  • SUS score: 88/100
  • NPS: +62
01 — Research

Mapping the chaos

I spent the first three weeks deeply embedded in the problem: living with participants in their smart homes, shadowing their daily routines, and logging every friction point. I also conducted 24 remote interviews with smart home enthusiasts and casual users.

Contextual inquiry revealed that the real problem wasn't just too many apps — it was a lack of mental model. Users couldn't picture what their home's "state" was at any given moment. The solution needed to create a coherent mental model, not just a pretty dashboard.

02 — Architecture

Organising by place, not product

The key insight from research: users think about their home in rooms and activities, not device categories. Reorganising the IA around spaces (Living Room, Bedroom, Kitchen) rather than device types (Lights, Locks, Cameras) reduced cognitive load dramatically.

I introduced "Scenes" — one-tap presets that configure multiple devices simultaneously for common scenarios (Morning, Movie Night, Away). Testing showed scenes became the most-used feature within the first week of use.

Mobile App · Mental Health · 2023

Sage Journal

An AI-powered journaling app that helps users understand their emotional patterns, recognise triggers, and build genuine resilience.

My Role Lead UX Designer
Timeline 16 Weeks
Tools Figma, Maze, Lottie, Principle
Platform iOS
The Challenge

Journaling apps that don't understand emotions

Mental health apps often take one of two approaches: clinical (cold CBT worksheets) or superficial (generic affirmations). Neither truly helps users understand themselves. The most valuable insight — pattern recognition across time — was either completely absent or buried in data visualisations users couldn't interpret.

Sage was designed to be something more: a companion that reads between the lines, surfaces insights gently, and helps users make sense of their emotional lives without doing the therapeutic work for them.

"How do we use AI to help people feel more human, not less?"

Design Challenges
  • Safe, non-clinical tone of voice
  • AI transparency without complexity
  • Privacy-first architecture
  • Trauma-informed design principles
  • Accessibility for vulnerable users
Collaborators
  • Clinical psychologist advisor
  • AI/NLP engineering team
  • Content & ethics review board
  • Trauma-informed UX consultant
01 — Ethics First

Designing for vulnerability

Before a single wireframe was drawn, I spent four weeks working with a clinical psychologist and an ethics board to establish design principles for a mental health app. This was not typical UX research — it was a fundamental rethinking of what responsible design means when users are at their most vulnerable.

🛡
Privacy by default

All entries encrypted on-device. AI processing occurs locally when possible. No user data used for model training without explicit, informed consent.

🌱
No gamification of wellbeing

No streaks, no leaderboards, no "you missed 3 days!" notifications. Mental health is not a game. Consistency is encouraged gently, never enforced.

🤲
Always offer support

Any entry containing distress signals (validated NLP triggers) presents a gentle check-in and access to crisis resources — without alarming the user.

💡
Transparent AI

AI insights are clearly labelled, always optional, and framed as observations — never diagnoses. "It seems like..." not "You are...".

02 — Design

Warmth in every pixel

The visual design needed to feel like a warm, dimly lit room — safe, private, unhurried. I moved away from the bright whites and sharp contrasts typical of productivity apps, choosing instead deep purples, warm creams, and gentle amber accents.

Typography choices leaned into editoriality — Cormorant Garamond for headings giving the journal a handwritten quality without being decorative to the point of illegibility. Generous line heights and white space created room to breathe.

03 — Testing

Getting feedback on sensitive ground

Usability testing a mental health app requires careful handling. I partnered with a clinical psychologist for all test sessions and implemented a comprehensive debrief protocol. Participants were screened to ensure they weren't in crisis, and all sessions included an emotional check-in at the end.

We ran 5 rounds of testing with 8 participants each, focusing on three core flows: entry creation, insight review, and crisis support discovery. The biggest learning: users wanted even more transparency about how the AI worked, not less.

Web Platform · Environment · 2024

Terra

A personal carbon footprint tracker that transforms complex environmental data into clear, actionable steps anyone can take.

My Role UX/UI Lead · Design System
Timeline 22 Weeks
Tools Figma, Framer, Lookback
Platform Web (Mobile-first)
The Challenge

Climate data that causes anxiety instead of action

Climate change communication has a well-documented problem: the scale of the crisis is so vast that people either disengage (overwhelm) or overestimate their individual impact (guilt without guidance). Existing carbon calculators produced a number — but no meaningful next step.

Terra needed to be different: a platform that made environmental action feel personal, manageable, and — crucially — positive. Change through empowerment, not shame.

"How do we make climate action feel personal, not paralysing?"

Scope
  • Carbon footprint calculator
  • Personalised action recommendations
  • Progress tracking over time
  • Community challenges
  • Offset marketplace integration
  • Business API (B2B tier)
Awards & Recognition
  • Webby Award nominee 2024
  • UX Design Award — Bronze
  • Featured in FastCompany
01 — Research

Understanding why people don't act

I ran a mixed-methods research programme: a survey of 400 participants across age groups, a 3-week diary study with 15 participants tracking their environmental decision-making, and 20 in-depth interviews with both climate-engaged and climate-disengaged people.

The central finding was counterintuitive: the people most disengaged were not climate deniers — they were people who cared deeply but felt their actions were pointless compared to corporate and government inaction. The design problem was restoring agency, not building awareness.

😰 Paralysis by scale

73% of climate-aware participants felt personal actions were "meaningless" against systemic issues. This belief was the primary barrier to any action at all.

🔢 Number blindness

"I've avoided carbon calculators because they just make me feel terrible and I don't know what to do next." — 11 of 20 interview participants expressed this sentiment.

🤝 Social proof matters

When participants learned that someone like them had made a specific change, the likelihood of adoption increased by 3–4× compared to reading about abstract impact statistics.

02 — Design Framework

Three principles that guided everything

🎯
Personal, not global

Every metric is contextualised to the user's life. "Your travel footprint is 2× higher than similar people in your city" hits harder than abstract CO₂ numbers.

✅
Actions over guilt

Every piece of data is paired with 1–3 specific, achievable actions. The tone is always empowering, never accusatory.

📈
Visible progress

Users can see their footprint reduce over time, creating a positive feedback loop. Momentum is more motivating than perfection.

03 — The Dashboard

Data as a tool, not a verdict

The dashboard was the hardest problem to solve. Early versions showed total carbon numbers prominently — this tested poorly. Users immediately felt judged. The solution: lead with trend and momentum, not absolute numbers. The most important thing isn't where you are — it's which direction you're moving.
